Albert Szent-Györgyi

The man, who discovered Vitamin C

His personal recollection Some reminiscences of my life as a scientist was published in 1976 in the International Journal of Quantum Chemistry.

Biography

Albert Szent-Györgyi was born in 1893 in Budapest, in the Kingdom of Hungary.
He began his undergraduate studies at the Institute of Anatomy at the University of Budapest, and then continued at the Institute of Physiology. ...as a third year medical student, I became increasingly discontent with morphology which told me little about life."-he wrote in his memoir.

During the First World War, he served as an army medic, and although he was awarded the Silver Medal of Merit he strongly disapproved of the bloodshed. After the war, due to the complicated political situation, he relocated his research to Prague, later to Germany and then to the Netherlands. In the article about the cellular respiration of plants - he resolved the debate that raged between O. Warburg and Wieland. It was then that the name Albert Szent-Györgyi became widely known in the academic world.

With the help of the recognised English biochemist Sir Frederick G. Hopkins, he was able to continue his work in Cambridge. He isolated an organic reducing agent which he called "hexuronic acid". Cambridge awarded him a PhD for the work in 1927.
He moved to the United States to isolate more of the substance from the adrenal gland of animals.

He invented many names for the so far unknown substance, like "ignose" (meaning of ignosco: I don't know) and 'godnose' (god knows). At the millennium the compound "hexuronic acid" was believed to be the same as ascorbic acid. This is no longer the case. Later Szent-Györgyi at the urging of his fellow colleague renamed it to ascorbic acid.

In 1928 he was appointed rector of the Miklós Horthy University, but left for Cambridge to complete his research. In 1930 he returned to Hungary and took up the position offered at the university. He soon established a reputation for fascinating lectures and an informal leadership style. He also continued his research on ascorbic acid and isolated it from paprika peppers.

The discovery of vitamin C has been the subject of research by scientists from all over the world.

The ensuing scientific debate about the priority of the discovery was resolved in 1937, when Szent-Györgyi received the Nobel Prize. Later offered the prize annuities to the victims of the Soviet-Finnish War (1939).

In his later research he focused on the production of usable energy by the cell. He recognised that some organic acid plays a major role in biological oxidation, and came close to discovering the basic process of cellular respiration: the citrate cycle. His work was groundbreaking and helped Hans Kerbs to identify all the components of the cycle. The process is today also known as the Krebs cycle or the Szent-Györgyi and Krebs cycle.

During the years of World War II, he was actively involved in the underground anti-fascist movement and was persuaded by the then Hungarian Prime Minister Miklós Kállay to travel to Istanbul and begin secret negotiations with the Allies. After Hungary's failure of the peaceful leaving of the Axis powers, he was forced to go into hiding. After the war, he undertook an active role in the political and academic life, among other things founded the Hungarian Academy of Natural Sciences. The Institute served as an intellectual centre for his fellow colleagues and apprentices and also was a home and shelter in the desperate times. The soon-to-come turn of political events forced him to leave his country: after his 1947 summer holiday in Switzerland he emigrated to the United States.

He established the Institute for Muscle Research at the Marine Biological Laboratory in Woods Hole, Massachusetts and started to research muscle tissues. Despite of the early financial difficulties he always managed to find supporters, even the death of his sponsor and friend left behind significant debt.

In the 1960s he became politically more active: he wrote numerous articles and gave lectures against the nuclear arms race and the Vietnam War.

After his second wife and only daughter died of breast cancer, he devoted the last two decades of his life to cancer research. Szent-Györgyi remained mentally and physically active in his last years. Only from 1985, at the age of 92, did his health visibly deteriorate. In June 1986 his condition suddenly worsened with kidney and heart failure. He underwent surgery and was discharged. A few months later, on 22 October, he died of renal failure at his home.

He is buried in Woods Hole Cemetery on the Atlantic coast.

Awards

  • 1916 - Silver Medal of Valour
  • 1937 - The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine
  • 1937 - Honorary citizen of the city of Szeged
  • 1938 - Honorary doctor of the University of Sorbonne
  • 1947 - Honorary doctor of the University of Oxford
  • 1948 - Kossuth Prize
  • 1954 - Albert Lasker Award
  • 1967 - Modern Medicine Award
